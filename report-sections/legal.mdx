A property's market value means nothing if its legal status is unclear. Before any transaction completes, you need to know who actually owns the property, whether any debts or claims are attached to it, and whether any restrictions limit what you can do with it. The legal verification section of your Estify AI report checks Poland's official property register so you don't have to navigate the system yourself — and so you don't get caught off guard at the notary's table.

## What Is the Księga Wieczysta?

The **Księga Wieczysta (KW)** — Poland's Land and Mortgage Register — is the official, legally binding record of a property's status. It is maintained by regional courts (*sądy rejonowe*) and is publicly accessible online through the Ministry of Justice portal. Every registered property in Poland has a unique KW number, and the register is organised into four parts:

  <Card title="Part I — Property Description" icon="map-pin">
    Records the property's physical details: address, land area or apartment surface, intended use (residential, commercial, agricultural), and any associated rights to shared infrastructure.
  </Card>
  <Card title="Part II — Ownership" icon="user-check">
    Lists the legal owner or co-owners, their ownership shares (e.g. 50/50 for a married couple), and the legal basis by which ownership was acquired (purchase, inheritance, court ruling).
  </Card>
  <Card title="Part III — Rights, Claims & Limitations" icon="scale-balanced">
    Records easements (e.g. a right of way across the plot), pre-emption rights held by third parties, and any court-ordered restrictions on the property's use or sale.
  </Card>
  <Card title="Part IV — Mortgages & Encumbrances" icon="lock">
    Lists all mortgages registered against the property, including the creditor (bank), the amount secured, and whether the mortgage has been released. Outstanding entries here mean the property carries debt.
  </Card>
</CardGroup>

## What Estify Checks

The legal verification module in your Estify report retrieves and analyses the relevant KW entries and flags the following:

- **Ownership status**: confirms whether the seller named in the listing matches the registered owner, and identifies any co-ownership situations that require additional consent
- **Mortgage encumbrances (Part IV)**: identifies any active mortgages, their amounts, and whether they have been formally released (*wykreślenie hipoteki*)
- **Legal claims and court restrictions (Part III)**: surfaces any pending litigation, bailiff notices, or court-imposed limitations on the property
- **Use restrictions**: flags easements or rights of way that could affect how you use or develop the property
- **Ownership history anomalies**: highlights recent or rapid ownership changes that may warrant closer scrutiny

## What the Legal Section Can Reveal

Not every legal issue is immediately visible from a listing or a property visit. The register check can surface:

- **Outstanding mortgages** that the seller has not disclosed — these transfer with the property unless cleared before completion
- **Contested ownership** where multiple parties claim a share, for example following an unresolved inheritance
- **Easements** that grant third parties ongoing access to part of the land or building
- **Pre-emption rights** held by local authorities, neighbours, or co-owners that give them the right to match your offer before the sale can proceed
- **Bailiff notices** (*wzmianki komornicze*) indicating the property is subject to enforcement proceedings against the current owner

<Warning>
  Purchasing a property with unresolved legal encumbrances can expose you to significant financial and legal risk. An outstanding mortgage registered in Part IV does not disappear on sale unless explicitly discharged — the bank can enforce its security against the new owner. Always ensure Part IV is clear, or that a structured discharge is agreed as a condition of completion.
</Warning>

## Do You Need the KW Number?

The KW number is the most direct way to retrieve a property's register entry, but you don't always need to provide it. In most cases, Estify can locate the correct register entry using the **property address and cadastral data** — the same information used in the transaction listing. If you do have the KW number (it appears on previous sale contracts, the listing itself, or the owner can provide it), entering it ensures an exact match and speeds up the retrieval.

If the property does not yet have a separate KW entry — for example, a new-build apartment sold off-plan — the report will flag this and note that the legal check will need to be revisited once the unit is formally registered.

<Tip>
  The legal section of your Estify report is an excellent starting point, but it is not a substitute for a notary (*notariusz*). Polish law requires all property transfers to be executed before a notary, who is legally obliged to verify the KW and confirm that the transaction is unencumbered. Use the Estify legal check early in your due diligence to decide whether to proceed — and bring any flagged issues to your notary's attention before signing anything.
</Tip>

## Reading Your Legal Risk Summary

In the Pro report, each legal finding is assigned a risk level:

| Risk Level | What It Means |
|---|---|
| **Clear** | No issues found; the register appears clean and consistent |
| **Advisory** | Minor entries present (e.g. an expired easement) that are unlikely to block a transaction but should be reviewed |
| **Caution** | Active encumbrances or claims that require resolution before or at completion |
| **High Risk** | Serious issues such as contested ownership, active enforcement proceedings, or undischarged mortgages that may make the transaction inadvisable without specialist legal advice |

If your report returns a Caution or High Risk rating in any category, treat it as a prompt to seek qualified legal counsel before proceeding — not after.
