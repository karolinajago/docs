What surrounds a property today is not necessarily what will surround it in five years. A quiet plot of land across the street might be designated for a twelve-storey residential tower. A greenfield site at the edge of the neighbourhood might be earmarked for a new tramway depot. Conversely, a neglected district might be on the cusp of a major regeneration programme that could significantly lift values. The spatial and zoning section of your Estify AI Pro report gives you access to this forward-looking planning data so you can make decisions based on what a location is becoming, not just what it is today.

<Note>
  The spatial and zoning section is available in the **Pro report** and draws on local authority planning databases, the national GIS planning portal (GUGiK), and regional spatial planning documents.
</Note>

## Poland's Planning System: MPZP and WZ

To understand this section of your report, it helps to know how Polish spatial planning works. There are two key instruments:

<CardGroup cols={2}>
  <Card title="MPZP — Local Development Plan" icon="map">
    The *Miejscowy Plan Zagospodarowania Przestrzennego* is a binding local by-law adopted by the municipal council. It defines precisely how each parcel of land in its coverage area can be used, what can be built, and to what height and density. It is the strongest form of planning certainty available.
  </Card>
  <Card title="WZ — Zoning Decision" icon="file-pen">
    The *Warunki Zabudowy* is an individual administrative decision issued for a specific development proposal in areas not covered by an MPZP. It is site-specific, can be challenged by third parties, and does not provide the same level of neighbourhood-wide planning certainty as a binding local plan.
  </Card>
</CardGroup>

Approximately **30–40% of Poland's urban land area** lacks an approved MPZP, meaning development in those areas is governed entirely by individual WZ decisions. This matters significantly when assessing what might be built near your property.

<Warning>
  Properties in areas **without an approved MPZP** (i.e. in WZ zones) carry greater planning uncertainty. A plot of green space next to your building may look like a permanent amenity, but without a binding local plan designating it as such, a developer can apply for — and potentially obtain — a WZ decision to build on it. Always check the planning status of adjacent land, not just the property itself.
</Warning>

## What the Report Covers

### Current Land Use Designation

For properties within an MPZP area, the report identifies the official land use designation for the subject property and its immediate surroundings. Common designations include:

| Code | Designation |
|---|---|
| MN | Single-family residential (*mieszkaniowa niska zabudowa*) |
| MW | Multi-family residential (*mieszkaniowa wielorodzinna*) |
| U | Commercial/services (*usługi*) |
| ZP | Public green space (*zieleń parkowa*) |
| KD | Roads and transport infrastructure |
| P | Industrial/production |

Understanding the designation of surrounding parcels tells you what can legally be built nearby — and what cannot.

### Nearby Planned Developments

The report flags approved or in-progress planning applications within a defined radius of the property, including:

- **Residential developments**: new apartment blocks or housing estates that may increase local supply and affect prices
- **Commercial and retail**: planned shopping centres, office parks, or service clusters — which can bring amenity but also traffic and noise
- **Infrastructure projects**: road widening schemes, new tram or metro lines, utility upgrades
- **Public facilities**: schools, hospitals, sports facilities — generally positive value drivers

### Building Height and Density Rules

Where an MPZP is in place, the report extracts the **maximum building height** (*maksymalna wysokość zabudowy*) and **plot coverage ratio** (*wskaźnik zabudowy*) for the local planning zone. This tells you how densely the area around your property can be developed, which is directly relevant to future views, sunlight access, and neighbourhood character.

### Planning Status Summary

The report clearly states whether the property sits within:
- An **approved MPZP** area (higher certainty)
- A **WZ decision** area (lower certainty)
- An area where a **new MPZP is in preparation** (transition zone — rules may change)

<Note>
  **MPZP vs. WZ — a practical difference:** If the plot next to your prospective apartment is covered by an MPZP designating it as ZP (public green space), you have strong grounds for certainty that it will remain green. If the same plot sits in a WZ zone with no binding designation, a developer can apply for permission to build on it at any time. Your Estify report tells you exactly which situation applies.
</Note>

## Why Spatial Planning Matters for Value

Planning designations are one of the most significant but least visible drivers of long-term property value. Consider these scenarios:

- A flat adjacent to a plot **designated for a new metro station** could see substantial value appreciation as construction progresses and connectivity improves
- An apartment next to land **re-designated from industrial to mixed-use residential** may benefit from improved neighbourhood amenity over 5–10 years
- A house on the edge of a city **where an MPZP re-designates agricultural land for residential** could be surrounded by dense new construction within a decade
- A property near a plot with an active **commercial development application** may face years of construction disruption, increased traffic, and a permanent change in local character

None of these scenarios are visible on a property listing. They become visible when you read the spatial and zoning section.

## Practical Example: Apartment Near a Commercial Designation

Consider a 60 m² apartment in a mid-sized Polish city, adjacent to a 0.8-hectare plot currently used as an informal car park. The Estify report reveals:

- The plot is designated **U (commercial/services)** in the binding local MPZP
- Maximum building height in the zone: 15m (approximately 4–5 storeys)
- An active planning application has been submitted for a **mixed-use building** with ground-floor retail and 40 residential units above
- Expected construction start: Q3 next year, based on the application timeline

**What this means in practice:** The buyer faces 18–24 months of construction disruption and a permanent change in the immediate view. On the positive side, the eventual addition of ground-floor retail amenity (café, pharmacy, grocery) may improve long-term desirability. The net effect on value depends on the quality of the completed development and the strength of demand in the area — both of which can be cross-referenced with the price forecast section of the report.

<Tip>
  If you are considering a property for **redevelopment or renovation** — for example, converting a commercial unit to residential, adding a rooftop extension, or subdividing a larger apartment — the spatial and zoning section is the first place to check. The MPZP designation and applicable building parameters will tell you what is legally permissible before you commission an architect or make any commitments. A WZ application may be possible in areas without a binding plan, but it adds time, cost, and uncertainty to your project.
</Tip>

## Reading the Planning Map in Your Report

The Pro report includes an **interactive planning overlay map** showing the subject property and surrounding land parcels colour-coded by their MPZP designation or WZ status. Each parcel is clickable to reveal its designation details, maximum build parameters, and any active planning applications.

Use this map to:
1. Identify the designation of every plot that directly borders your property
2. Check whether any adjacent parcels have active applications in the pipeline
3. Assess the overall balance of the neighbourhood — is it predominantly residential, mixed, or subject to significant commercial pressure?

Planning data is updated quarterly from municipal GIS sources. If you have reason to believe a significant new application has been submitted since your report date, you can verify current status directly through the relevant gmina's (municipality's) planning portal.
